Methodology White Paper

How HouseOffer decides whether a home is fairly priced

An asking price is a seller's opening position, not evidence of value. HouseOffer replaces that opinion with what comparable homes have actually sold for — using official HM Land Registry records and a transparent, repeatable method. This paper explains exactly how that method works, where the data comes from, and where its limits lie.

The problem

The asking price tells you what the seller hopes for, not what the home is worth

Estate agents are paid by sellers, and asking prices reflect that. A listing price is set to test the market, anchor negotiations, or win an instruction — it is rarely an independent estimate of fair value. Buyers, meanwhile, are expected to judge that price using the same listing, a few similar adverts, and instinct.

The evidence to do better is public and free. Every completed sale in England and Wales is recorded by HM Land Registry, and energy assessments for most homes sit in the EPC register. HouseOffer brings these official sources together and applies a consistent valuation method, so a buyer can see how an asking price compares with reality before making an offer.

Where the data comes from

Official records first, listings second

Every report is built from public, authoritative data sources:

  • HM Land Registry Price Paid Data — the definitive record of completed sale prices for residential property in England and Wales.
  • Recent comparable sales — nearby sold properties with their type, bedroom count, location and sale date, used to build a like-for-like comparison set.
  • The EPC register — energy certificates that supply floor area and, where they can be matched to the subject, help confirm the exact property.
  • The listing itself — asking price, property type, bedrooms and (where shown) floor area, taken from the advert the buyer is looking at.
  • House Price Index data — used to adjust older sales to present-day money, so a 2021 sale can be compared fairly with today's market.

We deliberately do not rely on a single black-box estimate. Each independent signal becomes one input into the blended valuation described below.

The core method

Bedroom-matched, distance-ranked comparable sales

Bedroom count and location are the two strongest drivers of price, so they sit at the heart of our comparable selection. Rather than averaging every sale in a postcode, we build a set of genuinely like-for-like homes around the subject property:

Match the property type
Only sales of the same normalised type (detached, semi, terraced, flat) are considered.
Match the bedroom count
We compare three-bed homes with three-bed homes — the single most decisive filter, and one that crude postcode averages ignore.
Filter by distance
We start from sales within a tight radius of the property and only widen if too few exist, keeping comparables genuinely local.
Rank by proximity and recency
Closer, more recent sales carry more weight; distant or older ones carry less.
Tighten by size where possible
When floor areas are available, we further restrict to homes within roughly ±20% of the subject's size.
Adjust to today and average
Each comparable is brought to present-day money using House Price Index data, then averaged using an interquartile mean that ignores extreme outliers.
Correctness over coverage

If too few like-for-like sales exist, we relax the filters in a deliberate order — first allowing ±1 bedroom, then a wider radius, and only as a last resort falling back to a broad area average. Whenever we widen, we lower the confidence we report, so a thin comparable set is flagged rather than hidden.

Triangulation

No single number — a weighted blend of independent methods

A robust valuation should not hang on one calculation. HouseOffer estimates value several different ways and combines them, weighting each by how reliable it is for the property in question. The result is presented as a range — a "football field" — rather than a single false-precision figure.

Comparable sales
Bedroom- and distance-matched sold prices, adjusted to today. The primary signal.
Area price trend
How local prices have moved over time for this type of home.
Price per square metre
Local £/m² benchmarks applied to the subject's floor area, where known.
Last sale, re-indexed
The property's own previous sale price, uplifted by the House Price Index.
Automated valuation
A model estimate using the property's attributes as a cross-check.
Estimated lender range (modelled)
A modelled estimate of how conservatively a mortgage surveyor might value, derived from our other methods — not sourced from lender data.

Where methods agree, confidence is high and the range is tight. Where they diverge, we widen the range and lower confidence rather than pretending to a precision the data cannot support.

Special cases

New-build homes are treated differently

Newly built homes typically command a premium over otherwise-similar resale properties, so valuing them purely on resale comparables would understate them. When a listing is a new build, we account for that premium and adjust the framing of the verdict accordingly — so the comparison is "new-build premium versus resale comparables", not a misleading "overpriced" flag.

Getting the right house

Honest about which property we are valuing

Listings often hide the exact house number, which matters because the wrong number means the wrong sale history. We only trust an exact-property match when an EPC or house-number match supports it. Where the exact home cannot be confidently identified, we say so and let you choose from the candidate addresses, rather than silently presenting a neighbour's sale as if it were the subject's.

This conservative approach occasionally means a little more work for the user, but it protects the one thing that matters most: that the numbers in your report belong to the property you are actually buying.

Limits and honesty

What this analysis is, and is not

HouseOffer is a fast, data-driven second opinion — not a physical inspection. Our analysis cannot see a property's condition, recent renovations, planning issues, lease terms, or kerb appeal, all of which can move real value. Public data also has gaps: some streets have no recent comparable sales, and some homes have no usable floor area on record. In those cases we lower the confidence we report rather than guess.

For these reasons, HouseOffer reports are informational only. They do not constitute financial advice, a formal RICS valuation, or a guarantee of price. Always commission a qualified surveyor before committing to a purchase.
